Vortex Operator Theory (VOT): A Dynamical Resonance Framework for the Zeros of the Riemann Zeta Function

Research preprint draft
HTML manuscript version • arXiv-style draft • empirical/theoretical hybrid document
Abstract

We introduce Vortex Operator Theory (VOT), a dynamical resonance framework for modeling the nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function. The guiding principle is that zeros should not be treated purely as static spectral locations, but as outcomes of evolving arithmetic resonance structures generated from a von Mangoldt-type signal and its local time-frequency geometry.

The resulting framework combines: (i) a damped prime-event signal, (ii) localized Fourier analysis, (iii) a relative purity surface penalizing leakage more strongly than raw amplitude, (iv) dynamic programming for extracting drifting key paths, and (v) a structured event system based on anchors, bridges, and transitions. We then reinterpret the resulting objects in a vortex ontology consisting of shells, a 0-point, and directed flow.

Empirically, the framework suggests that zeta zeros are not homogeneous. Instead, they appear to split into distinct mechanism classes, including stable shell-contact events and shell-approach events. A hybrid model combining a global scaffold with local correction layers improves empirical coverage relative to purely local models, while preserving a coherent vortex interpretation.

This document does not claim a proof of the Riemann Hypothesis. Rather, it presents a structured research program, a formal language for the model, and an empirical architecture that may help organize future work toward a deeper operator-theoretic or dynamical understanding of zeta zeros.

1. Introduction

Let ζ(s) denote the Riemann zeta function, and let its nontrivial zeros be written as

ρ = β + iγ.

The Riemann Hypothesis asserts that all nontrivial zeros satisfy

β = 1/2.

In classical treatments, zeta zeros are studied through analytic continuation, explicit formulas, spectral analogies, and deep relations with prime distributions. The present work proposes a complementary viewpoint:

Guiding Hypothesis.

Nontrivial zeta zeros may be viewed as outputs of a dynamical resonance system whose local structures are better described as evolving arithmetic flows than as isolated static targets.

The point of departure is not a direct reformulation of the classical analytic problem, but rather the construction of an arithmetic signal from prime events and the study of its local spectral organization. What emerged from this program was not a single universal resonance score, but a family of layered local structures. This led to a shift away from one-mechanism modeling and toward a multi-mechanism interpretation.

2. Conceptual Principle

The original working intuition of VOT can be summarized in three words:

shell — 0-point — flow.
Definition 2.1 (Shell).

A shell is a local resonance structure in the time-frequency geometry of the arithmetic signal. Intuitively, a shell is a coherent layer or band along which the signal maintains partial dynamical consistency.

Definition 2.2 (0-point).

A 0-point is a local attractor-like center toward which resonance alignment may evolve. It is not assumed to be a literal zero of the signal itself; rather, it is a dynamical center governing approach, locking, or failure.

Definition 2.3 (Flow).

A flow is an oriented local motion in the shell structure indicating whether the system is approaching a 0-point, stabilizing near it, or transitioning away from one shell toward another.

The zeta zeros are then interpreted not as arbitrary output points but as locations where one of a small number of local dynamical mechanisms becomes active.

3. Arithmetic Signal Construction

The signal used in the current VOT prototype is built from prime-power events. Let each prime event occur at time

τ = k \log p, \qquad p \text{ prime}, \; k \in \mathbb{N}.

In its simplest single-channel form, the signal is

A_0(T) = \sum_{τ \le T} \log p \, e^{-\alpha(T-τ)} \sin(\omega τ + \phi).

In later versions, multiple channels are allowed:

A_0^{(M)}(T) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \sum_{τ \le T} c_m \log p \, e^{-\alpha_m(T-τ)} \sin(\omega_m τ + \phi_m).

Here:

  • αm controls memory decay,
  • ωm is a channel frequency,
  • φm is a phase shift,
  • cm is a channel weight.
Remark.

A central empirical discovery was that a purely local flexible multi-component model improved low-zero fitting but often lost long-range stability, while an earlier simpler model scaled better at larger heights. This led to the current V1-backed hybrid architecture: a global scaffold plus local refinement.

4. Localized Spectral Geometry

The signal is analyzed locally via a Gaussian-windowed Fourier transform. For center time \(T\) and frequency variable \(ω\),

S(T,ω) = \int A_0(t)\, e^{iω t}\, \exp\!\left(-\frac{(t-T)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\,dt.

In computation, this is discretized over a time grid and a finite frequency interval. The main object of interest is the amplitude surface

|S(T,ω)|.

This surface is not used directly as a raw score. Instead, VOT studies how local energy concentrates into shells and how that concentration evolves through time.

5. Purity Surface and Dynamic Selection

A key lesson from development was that frequency correctness matters more than raw amplitude. Accordingly, the central derived surface is not amplitude itself but a relative purity surface.

In the basic form, one measures:

  • local concentration: how much energy lies near a candidate frequency,
  • leakage: how much energy spills away from that neighborhood,
  • cleanup: whether mismatch is dynamically decreasing,
  • asymmetry: a weaker shape descriptor.

One generic form used during development is:

P(T,ω) = a_1 \,\text{energy}(T,ω) + a_2 \,\text{concentration}(T,ω) + a_3 \,\max(\text{cleanup}(T,ω),0) - a_4 \,\text{leakage}(T,ω) + a_5 \,\text{asymmetry}(T,ω).

From this surface, drifting key paths are extracted by dynamic programming:

DP[i,j] = \max_k \left( DP[i-1,k] + P[i,j] - \lambda |j-k| \right).

This produces frequency paths that are locally coherent but not rigidly fixed.

6. Event Ontology

VOT does not reduce the analysis to a single curve. Instead, the extracted layered structures generate multiple event types.

Definition 6.1 (Anchor / Visible Contact).

An anchor is a local event corresponding to stable shell contact: high local purity, a localized peak, and sufficient persistence.

Definition 6.2 (Bridge / Weak Attempt).

A bridge is a weaker local event corresponding to directed approach toward shell contact. It is not accepted automatically: it must contribute genuinely new zero coverage or explanatory value.

Definition 6.3 (Transition).

A transition is a local structural switch in the resonance geometry. In the current formulation it is not, by default, treated as a standalone event; instead it acts mainly as a trigger or support condition.

Zero-Zone Principle.

The model evaluates explanatory success through zero-zones, i.e. event unions with widths, rather than point hits. A zero is counted as explained when it lies within a prescribed distance threshold from the selected event union.

7. Vortex Interpretation

To prevent the model from becoming a mere detector, an explicit vortex interpretation was imposed back on top of the event system.

Each event receives derived vortex descriptors:

  • shell_id
  • shell_stability
  • zero_point_approach_score
  • zero_point_lock_score
  • shell_transition_score
  • vortex_role
  • vortex_valid

In current usage, the key role classes are:

  • shell_contact: stable shell contact near a 0-point,
  • shell_approach: internal approach toward a 0-point,
  • shell_transition: shell switch,
  • shell_failure: insufficient vortex consistency.
Interpretive Proposition.

The anchor/bridge distinction is not merely algorithmic. Under the VOT interpretation, anchors correspond primarily to shell-contact mechanisms, whereas bridges correspond to shell-approach mechanisms. This gives a mechanistic reading to event heterogeneity.

8. Hybrid Architecture

A major empirical turning point occurred when it was observed that the earlier model version V1 appeared to scale better with increasing zero height than later more flexible local models. This led to the present architecture:

final model = V1 backbone + local precision/rescue correction.

The architecture is organized into four modes:

  • V1: global scaffold / long-range backbone,
  • precision: sharp local correction,
  • rescue: flexible recovery in difficult regions,
  • local_rescue: tightly constrained targeted patch for rare hard clusters.

The important conceptual point is that these are not viewed as arbitrary fitting modes but as distinct explanatory mechanisms in the evolving resonance field.

9. Zero Classification Layer

To move beyond coverage-only evaluation, a Zero Classification Layer was introduced. Each zero is mapped to:

  • a mode: V1, precision, rescue, or local_rescue,
  • a vortex role: shell_contact, shell_approach, shell_transition, shell_failure,
  • a shell label: S1–S5.
Empirical Classification Summary (first 20 zeros).
  • Modes: precision 14, rescue 4, local_rescue 2.
  • Roles: shell_contact 17, shell_approach 3.
  • The shell_approach subgroup is small but persistent, suggesting a distinct dynamical class rather than noise.

This is one of the strongest conceptual results of the project so far: zeros do not behave as a homogeneous family in the VOT framework.

10. Empirical Scaling Summary

A staged scaling test was performed at 20, 100, 300, and 1000 zeros. The best large-scale behavior among current variants came from the V1-backed hybrid.

Caution.

These results do not constitute a proof, a validated predictive model, or a numerically complete theory. They indicate only that the V1-backed hybrid organizes the observed low-to-mid-height structure better than several simpler alternatives explored in the project.

11. Interpretive Claims and Limits

11.1 What is being claimed

  • The VOT program provides a coherent dynamical language for discussing zeta-zero organization.
  • The empirical models support multi-mechanism behavior rather than one-mechanism uniformity.
  • The vortex ontology is not merely decorative; it tracks differences between stable contacts and approach events.
  • A global scaffold appears necessary for scaling, while local flexibility improves low-height explanatory power.

11.2 What is not being claimed

  • No proof of the Riemann Hypothesis is given.
  • No theorem yet shows that the present construction forces all zeros to lie on the critical line.
  • No uniqueness theorem yet identifies the VOT architecture as canonical.
  • The present work does not yet derive the observed structures from classical operator theory in a rigorous way.

12. Toward a Mathematical Program

The framework suggests several mathematical directions:

  1. Operator formalization. Identify a natural operator or semigroup whose local spectral geometry produces shell-contact and shell-approach classes.
  2. Shell invariants. Define quantities invariant under local refinement but preserved across large height scales.
  3. Blockwise classification. Extend the zero classification layer to higher zeros in blocks, testing whether the same mechanism taxonomy persists.
  4. Constraint theory. Replace empirical vortex tags by rigorous criteria defining when a local structure qualifies as shell contact, shell approach, or transition.
  5. Critical-line relation. Search for a mechanism showing that consistent shell-locking is only possible when the associated zero belongs to the critical line.
Programmatic Goal.

The long-term objective is not merely to fit known zeros, but to discover a structural principle under which zeta zeros become necessary outputs of a constrained arithmetic flow.

13. Conclusion

Vortex Operator Theory proposes that the zeros of the Riemann zeta function may be better understood as outputs of a layered arithmetic resonance dynamics than as isolated static points. The framework now contains:

  • a prime-event signal construction,
  • a local spectral geometry,
  • a purity-based path extraction mechanism,
  • an event ontology,
  • a vortex interpretation,
  • a hybrid architecture with a global scaffold,
  • and a zero classification layer.

The most important conceptual conclusion at present is that zeta zeros appear, within this framework, to be mechanistically heterogeneous. Some behave as stable shell-contact events, others as shell-approach events, and a small minority require local rescue structures. This strongly suggests that any successful future theory may have to explain a family of resonance mechanisms rather than a single universal template.

The present document should therefore be read as a preprint-style research program: partly empirical, partly formal, and explicitly incomplete—but organized enough to be criticized, extended, and tested.
